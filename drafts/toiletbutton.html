Toilet buttons05 Dec 2024

Here's a simple question: given a dual flush toilet, which button would you press for the "small flush"?

I posed this question to my followers on Instagram, and after a day, I received a unanimous vote for the "small button". Some even replied to my story, expressing their ___ at how obvious the choice should be. In the spirit of research, I told them to "just vote".

Well, that makes sense. So why is it that I sometimes reach for the wrong button? It's funny on several ends: First, well, my household has dual flush toilets installed (practically universal in all Singaporean households). Secondly, I definitely use the toilet a healthy amount, which would be multiple times every single day for the past many years I've been on this good Earth. These all mean that I should, by now, have already developed the knowledge, let alone the muscle memory, to press the right button. I should mention I have been toilet trained since young and am in no manner, as one might put it, developmentally challenged.

I am led to believe that I am subscribed to conflicting schools of thought, in particular of design, that create this

Interaction Design

This is a physical application of Fitt's Law, which states that the time taken to move to a target (say, a button) is dependent on the distance to the target and the width of the target. In essence, bigger buttons are easier to hit.

The law manifests in interaction design all the time. Just looking at interfaces, there's a reason why the "BUY" button on an online store can take up a quarter of the page, while the "X" on an advertisement is not only tiny but also in one far-reaching corner,] you could even misclick it and click on the ad instead. These are both ways that designers ease user behaviour towards or against a certain behaviour.

I think the next step is to break down why toilet buttons are so taken for granted!
